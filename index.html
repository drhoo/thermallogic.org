Beyond cooling.

Thermal Logic explores whether unwanted heat can be reduced, conditioned, redirected, converted, or partially cancelled closer to its point of origin.

Modern engineering is highly developed at moving heat. We are interested in the deeper question: can heat be treated as a controllable system behaviour, not merely a waste product to be transported elsewhere?

The question

For centuries we have become better at generating, transferring, storing, insulating, and rejecting heat. Thermal Logic asks whether the next step is not simply better cooling, but a better design language for unwanted heat itself.

01

Heat as origin

At what moment does useful energy become unwanted thermal disorder, and can that transition be altered?

02

Heat as circuit

Can thermal systems be treated with equivalents of resistance, capacitance, rectification, switching, oscillation, resonance, and conversion?

03

Heat as pattern

Before heat becomes low-grade waste, does it retain exploitable structure through gradients, materials, surfaces, phonons, radiation, or phase behaviour?

Research paths

The project begins by mapping existing work, forgotten ideas, adjacent fields, and practical mechanisms that may help move thermal thinking beyond conventional heat transfer.

Heat-origin designReducing or shaping waste heat at the point where useful energy degrades into thermal disorder.
Thermal circuitsExploring thermal analogues of components used in electrical systems: storage, direction, switching, buffering, oscillation, and conversion.
Phonons and vibrationInvestigating whether lattice vibration and material structure can be filtered, scattered, biased, or converted more intelligently.
Resonance and oscillationLearning from thermoacoustics, regenerators, caloric effects, standing waves, and cyclic thermal systems.
Directional heat flowThermal diodes, rectifiers, anisotropic materials, layered surfaces, and geometry that can bias heat pathways.
Conversion and recoveryThermoelectrics, radiative cooling, phase-change materials, heat batteries, and methods for capturing useful work from gradients.

Why now?

Excess heat is a shared constraint across data centres, chips, batteries, buildings, transport, industrial systems, and energy infrastructure. As machines become denser and more capable, unwanted heat becomes a hard limit, not a side issue.

Scale

Small gains matter

At modern infrastructure scale, even modest thermal improvements can affect energy demand, hardware life, operating cost, and system reliability.

Limit

Cooling is mature

Fans, pumps, liquid loops, heat exchangers, and thermal transfer systems are highly refined. That maturity is precisely why a different frame may be needed.

Frame

From herding to control

Thermal Logic explores whether heat can move from being a beast to be herded into a behaviour that can be patterned, conditioned, and controlled.
